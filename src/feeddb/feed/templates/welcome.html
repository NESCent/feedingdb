Welcome to FEED

{% endblock %} {% block content %}

Feeding is critical to survival and a behavior during which numerous anatomical structures function in complex ways. The evolution and diversification of mammals is a story that highlights changes in these structures over time. What we know about mammalian craniofacial evolution rests, in large part, on physiologic studies of the motor patterns of oral-pharyngeal muscles during feeding, and on the movements and forces within the feeding apparatus during these behaviors. Despite the pivotal role of feeding in mammalian evolution, broad-scale physiological analyses of phenotypic diversity and evolution are rare across vertebrates.

The Feeding Experiments End-user Database, or FEED, is a first step in providing the cyberinfrastructure so that experimental biologists studying organismal-level processes can synthesize data on feeding physiology and describe the functional, evolutionary and adaptive implications of this physiologic variation. FEED is a growing collection of physiologic data and associated metadata for a number of species. The physiologic data contain information on muscle activity, bone and muscle strain, jaw and oropharyngeal apparatus motion, and intra-oral pressure during feeding behaviors.

FEED is publically-available and the code is open source. It incorporates muscle anatomy and feeding behaviors ontologies. The data are searchable and can be downloaded in CSV format.

First-time visitors should read About FEED and will need to register before downloading or uploading data. If you use data from this website, please cite feedexp.org.
